Wave-Particle Duality

← Back to Modern Physics

The De Broglie hypothesis suggests that particles such as electrons exhibit wave-like behavior and are associated with a wavelength. Think of it like this: every moving particle is “surfing” on its own wave — but unless it’s really tiny, the wave is too small to notice!

De Broglie’s hypothesis unified the particle and wave nature of matter, suggesting that not only light but also matter exhibits dual behavior.

The Equation

According to De Broglie, the wavelength \( \lambda \) associated with a particle is given by:

\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} \]

  • \( \lambda \): De Broglie wavelength
  • \( h \): Planck’s constant (\(6.63 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J·s}\))
  • \( p \): momentum of the particle (\(mv\) in non-relativistic scenarios)

This means that all matter has wave-like properties, though the effect is only noticeable for very small particles like electrons and atoms. For large objects, the wavelength is extremely small and practically undetectable.

Applications

  • Electron diffraction experiments confirmed the wave nature of matter.
  • Used in designing electron microscopes, which rely on the wave behavior of electrons to achieve high resolution.
